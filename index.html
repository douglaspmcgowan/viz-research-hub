00 · Introduction

The question behind this site

How do you arrange a body of thought — papers, ideas, people, talks, half-formed notes — so that it stays thinkable as it grows? This document collects everything humans have tried: reactive prose, scroll-driven essays, force-directed graphs, embedding galaxies, infinite canvases, explorable simulations, and the zoomable-universe metaphor that underlies the richest of these tools.

Three questions shape the sections below:

  • How do we explain — essays that react to a reader's manipulations, simulations that you play with to understand, reactive numbers that cascade through prose.
  • How do we explore — graphs, maps, embedding spaces, infinite canvases, and semantic zoom across multiple levels of aggregation.
  • How do we communicate — visual essays, scrollytelling, narrative martini-glasses, and the aesthetic traditions of data art.
The four-level vision driving this

The tool you’re imagining has four zoom tiers. Each tier is a different kind of interface, but they share one camera. The sections below exist in service of building this.

01

Universe

Your whole knowledge space, seen at once.

Clusters, constellations, colored density. Too far out to read labels of individual items — just the shapes of what you’ve been thinking about.

02

Cluster

A constellation resolves into its members.

One cluster is now the stage. You can see the papers, ideas, people inside it. Auto-labeled by topic. Edges suggest links.

03

Canvas

An infinite whiteboard for one territory of thought.

Cards arranged in groups, connected, annotated. Sticky notes, text blocks, inline quotes. You can rearrange and write here freely.

04

Detail

A single card opens into a full explorable page.

Source links, full notes, embedded visualizations, reactive variables, scrollytelling within a card. The “expanded” view of any node.

01 · Explorable Explanations

Documents that react to you

Bret Victor’s 2011 essay named the genre: prose whose statements change as you manipulate its premises. The idea branched — into playable essays (Nicky Case), interactive textbooks (Seeing Theory, Immersive Linear Algebra), and long-form explainers where every number is draggable.

The triad from Victor’s essay: reactive documents (statements that update when a premise changes), explorable examples (miniature sandboxes embedded inline), and contextual information (every claim tethered to its source). Most modern explorables combine all three.

I want to convince you that the current state of written media is embarrassing — not because it fails to use fancy new technology, but because it fails to use the most basic technology of all: reactive feedback. Bret Victor · Explorable Explanations · 2011
Live · Tangle-style demo

Drag the highlighted numbers

If 20% of US households cut their energy use by 10% , the country saves 2.00% of its total energy — the equivalent of 14.0 power plants shut down.

This is the simplest form of an explorable. Both variables drive a shared expression. Drag left or right on the highlighted numbers. The pattern is from Tangle.js.

Techniques used

  • Reactive variablesTangle-style inline numbers that cascade through the whole document.
  • Inline widgetsMiniature playable sims interleaved with text (ncase, Setosa).
  • Click-to-advanceReader paces through frames of an animation (Polygons, R2D3).
  • Parameter slidersKnobs you can turn on an embedded system (TF Playground, GAN Lab).
  • Agent-based modelsCells or creatures following rules you edit live.
  • Before/after slidersJuxtaposition comparators.
  • Direct-manipulation geometryDrag control points to reshape a curve or diagram.
  • Embedded spaced repetitionFlashcards inline in prose (Quantum Country, Orbit).
  • Linked primary sourcesEvery number is a live citation that opens on hover.
  • Playable essaysA whole argument delivered as a short game.

A warning worth remembering

Explorables are expensive to make — Bartosz Ciechanowski’s articles take months each. Distill paused in 2021 citing author burnout. Techniques

  • Sticky graphic + stepped textGraphic pins, text-steps swap state. The dominant pattern.
  • Parallax scrollingLayers move at different speeds for depth.
  • Scroll-driven animationScroll position fires discrete animation frames.
  • Map-based scrollyScroll drives a zooming, panning map.
  • Video-scrubbing scrollyScroll scrubs currentTime on an HTML5 video.
  • Chapter transitionsFull-bleed slides swap at scroll thresholds.
  • Horizontal scrollyVertical scroll translated to horizontal movement.
  • 3D scrollytellingThree.js camera moves with scroll (Notre-Dame, Pudding globes).
  • Branching / game scrollyScroll + choice hybrids (Uber Game).
  • Dataset-as-protagonistA single dataset revealed progressively, overview to detail.

03 · Reactive Documents

Prose where every value is live

A reactive document is one where inputs change outputs automatically — the whole page is a spreadsheet in disguise. Tangle in 2011 invented the idiom; Observable generalized it into a full notebook; modern frameworks (Svelte, MDX, Solid) make it a language feature.

The core pattern

Why reactivity is the right default for research writing

When a number appears in your notes, it almost always depends on other numbers or assumptions. If you can make the derivation first-class — so the reader sees not just the figure but the formula it came from, and can poke the inputs — the document stops lying about false precision. This is Victor’s deep claim: reactive documents don’t just look cooler; they are more honest.

Reactive variables vs. reactive cells

  • Inline reactive valuesTangle, Nutshell — a number embedded in prose drags to change and cascades.
  • Reactive cellsObservable, marimo — whole chunks of code re-run in a dependency graph.
  • Reactive componentsSvelte, Solid, React signals — UI primitives that subscribe to data.
  • Reactive paragraphsExperimental — whole text blocks rewritten by a language model when inputs change.

If your personal site will hold explorables at all, pick one reactive substrate and commit. The library war here matters less than the consistency. Observable Framework is the least painful default for 2026.

Clustering algorithms that matter

  • LeidenCommunity detection. The modern successor to Louvain — produces better-connected communities. Use this.
  • LouvainClassic modularity-based clustering. Still everywhere; Leiden usually beats it.
  • HDBSCANDensity-based clustering. Produces hierarchical trees, handles noise, doesn’t force you to pick k.
  • k-meansFast, classic, requires k. Good for regular-sized clusters, bad for irregular ones.
  • ForceAtlas2A layout algorithm, not a clustering one, but it reveals cluster structure spatially. Gephi’s default.

Practical recipe: embed your items → reduce to 10D with UMAP → HDBSCAN on that 10D → UMAP again to 2D for display. The two-stage UMAP separates “what clusters with what” from “where do I put the dot” and produces visibly cleaner maps.

05 · Embedding Spaces

When meaning becomes geometry

An embedding is a learned vector that places every item somewhere in a high-dimensional space where “similar” means “nearby.” Reduce that space to 2D or 3D, and you get a literal map of your corpus — the thing that feels most like a galaxy.

The map is not the territory, but in an embedding space, the territory is a map. Loosely after Korzybski

Dimensionality reduction algorithms

  • UMAPUniform Manifold Approximation and Projection. The current default. Preserves both local and global structure better than t-SNE.
  • t-SNEOlder. Beautiful local clusters, poor global structure. Mostly superseded by UMAP.
  • PaCMAP / TriMapUMAP alternatives with better global structure claims. Worth trying if your UMAP maps feel wrong.
  • PCALinear. Fast. Interpretable axes. Bad for nonlinear manifolds but a necessary first check.
  • MDSClassical multidimensional scaling. Preserves pairwise distances. Slow but principled.
  • Isomap / LLEOlder manifold-learning methods. Still in sklearn; rarely the right answer now.

Embedding model options

Voyage AIvoyage-3 / voyage-3-large. Anthropic’s recommended embedding partner. Strong retrieval benchmarks.
Jina Embeddingsv3 text, v4 multimodal. Open weights, long context, strong for multilingual.

sentence-transformersThe Python library for local embeddings. Open-weight models, runs on CPU.
Nomic EmbedFully open-source (training data + weights) embedding model. 768d, 8K context.
MTEB LeaderboardRanked comparison of every embedding model on retrieval, clustering, classification benchmarks.
The critical move: hierarchical topic labels

A scatter of 100,000 dots is a blob. What makes Nomic Atlas feel like a map is that it computes clusters at multiple scales, picks a representative phrase for each, and shows those phrases at different zoom levels — just like cities vs. neighborhoods on Google Maps. The technique: HDBSCAN tree → top-k TF-IDF terms per cluster → label-at-zoom.

What “canvas” actually means

  • Infinite pan & zoomNo page boundary; the plane goes anywhere. Implemented via CSS transform or viewport math.
  • Free positioningObjects live at arbitrary (x, y). Not constrained to a grid or flow.
  • Edge toolsArrows, labels, sticky-end attachment to shapes that move.
  • Multi-selectionLasso, shift-click. Group moves. The affordance that makes rearrangement feel alive.
  • Nesting / framesBounded groups that contain other objects — in Figma, Muse, tldraw.
  • Semantic zoom(Rare but powerful) — cards show different content at different zoom levels.
Why this is the right substrate for level 3

The canvas gives you spatial memory — your brain genuinely encodes “the 2019 ethnography papers are in the top-right.” It also gives you ambient structure without forcing a schema. You can build groupings before you know how to name them. For research synthesis, this is priceless.

Techniques that make zoom feel like zoom

  • Tile pyramidsPrecompute images or vector data at each zoom level z. Serve only visible tiles.
  • Quadtree spatial indexingQuery “points visible at this viewport” in O(log n). How Deepscatter handles millions of dots.
  • SuperclusterMapbox’s hierarchical-cluster library for point data. Handles millions of markers.
  • Semantic zoomNot just bigger — different. A cluster at z=0 becomes a list of papers at z=5.
  • Level-of-detail (LOD) swapsDiscrete threshold: below k=2 show density heatmap, above show points.
  • FLIP animationsFirst-Last-Invert-Play. Animate layout changes by recording before/after and interpolating.
  • Matched geometryAn element transforms into a different element at another scale (iOS hero animations, Framer Motion layoutId).
  • Focus+contextFurnas’ Degree-of-Interest. Magnify the focus, compress the context. Fisheye views.
  • Progressive disclosureShneiderman’s mantra: overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand.

Furnas’ Degree-of-Interest, in plain English

For every item, compute DOI = Intrinsic importance − Distance from focus. Show items whose DOI exceeds some threshold. This is the math behind good focus+context views — it tells you which items deserve space at a given zoom, and which can compress into a cluster dot.

What makes these work

  • Logarithmic progressionLinear scroll, log-scale content. Each pixel roughly doubles the number.
  • Anchor items at every decadeSomething familiar at 10⁰, 10³, 10⁶ — otherwise you lose the reader.
  • Musical pacingNeal Agarwal uses ambient audio and cut-backs to break monotony.
  • No text until neededText appears only at its scale’s item. Silence in between.
  • One mechanicThe reader never has to learn a second input. Just keep scrolling / pressing right.
Why this belongs at level 1 of your tool

When a user first lands at the universe view, something cosmic should happen — a slow zoom-out from a single familiar idea (their most recent note) into the whole galaxy of their knowledge. Not interactive at that moment, just a short reveal. It primes the spatial metaphor that the rest of the interface trades on.

13 · Concepts & Principles

The ideas behind every decision

A glossary of the theoretical pieces that come up when you design one of these tools. Each has a one-line definition, the person or paper it’s from, and where it applies in your build.

Shneiderman’s Mantra “Overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand.” Ben Shneiderman, 1996, The Eyes Have It. Applies directly to the four-level zoom structure — you’re literally building an instance of it.
Degree of Interest (DOI) Furnas, 1986. DOI = Intrinsic Importance − Distance from Focus. Answers “which items deserve display at this altitude?” Use to decide what collapses into a cluster vs. stays visible.
Focus + Context Show the detail the user needs without losing the whole. Fisheye views, semantic zoom, and context-preserving navigation are all in this family.
Semantic Zoom From Pad++/Zoomable UIs research, Perlin & Fox. Objects don’t just scale — they swap representation at scale thresholds. Your cluster → papers transition is this.
Segel & Heer’s Narrative Visualization Taxonomy Martini Glass · Interactive Slideshow · Drill-Down Story. The canonical frame for where author control vs. reader control sit.
Explanatory vs. Exploratory Cole Knaflic’s distinction. Explanatory = you know the story, present it. Exploratory = the reader finds the story. Most research tools are exploratory with explanatory scaffolding.
“Show and Tell” Alberto Cairo. Good visualizations always pair visualization with annotation. Both modes — data and caption — carry meaning.
Data Humanism Giorgia Lupi, 2017 manifesto. A reaction against cold, anonymous charting. Invite imperfection, context, handwriting, emotion. Relevant because your research is yours — a humanist design register may beat a clinical one.
Tufte’s Data-Ink Ratio Maximize ink that encodes data; minimize everything else. A good check when your canvas gets cluttered with chrome.
Gestalt Grouping Principles Proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, common fate. Why clusters in your map feel like “a thing” when rendered well.
Progressive Disclosure UX principle from Don Norman et al. Reveal complexity only when the user reaches for it. The universe → cluster → canvas → detail path is progressive disclosure writ huge.
Reactive Document Bret Victor, 2011. A document whose outputs are functions of its inputs, where inputs are reader-manipulable. The substrate of explorables.
Explorable Explanation Bret Victor, 2011. Composed of reactive documents, explorable examples, and contextual information. The whole genre’s name.
“Kill Math” & its critique Victor’s claim that symbolic math fails human cognition; Evan Miller’s pushback that notation has irreplaceable power. The core tension every explorable designer wrestles with.
The Zoomable User Interface (ZUI) Perlin & Fox, Pad, 1993. The academic antecedent to Google Maps, Prezi, Figma, and the interface you’re building.
Small Multiples Tufte. Many small charts sharing the same axes. Good mode for comparing clusters or slices of your corpus at a glance.
Constructive Visualization Huron, Carpendale et al. Visualization-as-activity, where building the view is itself the learning. Close to what you want at the canvas level.
Distant Reading Franco Moretti. Reading a corpus at a scale no individual can read. The intellectual case for embedding maps as scholarly objects.
FLIP animation First-Last-Invert-Play, Paul Lewis. Record before/after bounding boxes, invert the difference, interpolate. How level transitions are made to feel continuous.
Shared Element Transition iOS / Android pattern. An element from view A morphs into the corresponding element in view B. In web, Framer Motion layoutId + the View Transitions API.
The one rule worth memorizing

At every zoom level of your tool, ask: “What is the minimum that makes this level legible on its own, and what lives at another level that this level should gesture toward?” Get both answers right and the whole stack feels inevitable.

14 · Your Build

Twelve ways to actually make the thing

Everything above was research. This section is construction. Twelve distinct paths to the four-level zoom tool, each a coherent stack with its own philosophy. Each option names the tradeoff it’s making — you’re picking which problem you’d rather solve, not which is “best.”

Before the options, the shared backbone. Almost every path below assumes roughly the same data pipeline:

The shared backend pattern

Ingest (PDFs via GROBID or Docling, Zotero via API, manual notes, OpenAlex for external papers) → Normalize (unified schema: item with type, title, text, source, tags) → Embed (Voyage or OpenAI or local sentence-transformers) → Reduce (UMAP to 10D then 2D) → Cluster (HDBSCAN on the 10D) → Label (c-TF-IDF or Claude on the cluster contents) → Store (SQLite + sqlite-vec, or DuckDB, or Postgres + pgvector) → Serve (a small JSON/Arrow API the front-end consumes).

This pipeline is identical across the options below. What changes is what you wrap it in.

Option 01

Observable Framework + Deepscatter

Easy

The “you already know the pipeline” path. Embrace it.

Observable Framework Deepscatter DuckDB-WASM Python dataloaders Markdown pages

What it looks like

A static site. Your universe view is a Deepscatter canvas reading a Parquet file of embeddings. Cluster labels come from a companion JSON. Each card’s detail page is a Markdown file with reactive cells that read from DuckDB-WASM. Canvas mode is a tldraw iframe embedded in a Markdown page.

Why pick it

Dataloaders in any language mean your Python pipeline is a first-class citizen. Zero server. Deploys to Vercel/Netlify. The explorable-explanations community’s own framework, so it thinks in the idioms of this site.

Where it hurts

Not built for full-app UIs. Level-3 canvas editing is awkward unless you embed something else. Routing / state across pages is minimal.

Pick this if

You want to ship something in a week and mostly write in Markdown. Add interactive surfaces later.

Option 02

Next.js + Cosmograph + tldraw

Medium

The “full product” path. The one most likely to turn into a real app.

Next.js (App Router) Cosmograph tldraw SDK Supabase Framer Motion Claude API

What it looks like

Auth via Supabase. Level 1 is a Cosmograph canvas reading from pgvector. Level 2 is the same Cosmograph zoomed, with cluster-labels fetched at higher LOD. Level 3 swaps to a tldraw canvas keyed by cluster ID. Level 4 is a Next.js route with MDX content. layoutId transitions between levels.

Why pick it

Every piece is actively maintained and mainstream. Deploys to Vercel with one button. Full backend via Supabase (auth, postgres, storage). Cosmograph’s GPU sim scales to your entire future library.

Where it hurts

Most code. You’re writing a real app. Theme consistency across Cosmograph + tldraw + your own pages takes work. Cosmograph’s license is MIT with some paid tiers for advanced features.

Pick this if

You want something you could eventually share with other researchers, and you’re willing to spend a few months.

Option 03

SvelteKit + Sigma.js + Pudding-style scrolly

Medium

The “make every detail page a Pudding essay” path.

SvelteKit Sigma.js + Graphology Scrollama D3 Turso (libSQL)

What it looks like

Universe and cluster views are Sigma + Graphology with Leiden clustering. Canvas is a Svelte-built grid-with-arrows. Detail pages use Scrollama to reveal the research story Pudding-style — sticky graphic, stepped annotations.

Why pick it

Svelte’s reactivity is tailor-made for reactive documents. Tiny bundles. The stack the Pudding already uses, so you’re mirroring the best visual-essay studio.

Where it hurts

Smaller ecosystem than React. Fewer ready-made components for canvas editing. You’ll either build the canvas in Svelte or embed something non-native.

Pick this if

The scrolly-essay detail pages matter most to you — the universe is nice but the stories it contains are the point.

Option 04

Astro + MDX + Islands

Easy

The “mostly a book, with widgets” path.

Astro MDX React / Svelte islands Static JSON D3

What it looks like

The whole site is Markdown/MDX. The universe and cluster views are React (or Svelte) “islands” that Astro hydrates selectively. Canvas is a tldraw iframe. Detail pages are MDX files rendered at build time.

Why pick it

Blistering fast. Almost no JS ships unless a visitor opens a widget. Great DX. Deploys anywhere — Vercel, Netlify, GitHub Pages, Cloudflare Pages.

Where it hurts

Adding/editing items means rebuilding the site. Not great once your corpus changes hourly. Full app UX (auth, real-time collab) is out of reach without adding services.

Pick this if

You edit in bulk, deploy occasionally, and mostly want the site to be a finished document.

Option 05

Three.js / R3F 3D Universe

Hard

The “lean into the cosmic metaphor” path.

react-three-fiber Drei Three.js postprocessing Next.js Framer Motion 3D

What it looks like

Level 1 is a literal 3D starfield — your embeddings rendered as stars in 3D space with a fly-through camera. Cluster is a zoom-in to a local region with star labels. Canvas is either a 2D HTML overlay or a flat plane in the scene. Detail is a full-screen HTML card.

Why pick it

Incredibly evocative. Closest in feel to “100,000 Stars,” “Size of Space,” and the neal.fun aesthetic you cited. Potentially unforgettable as a landing experience.

Where it hurts

Hardest of the twelve. 3D labels, depth-aware interaction, text legibility in 3D, mobile performance — every one of these is a multi-week problem. Accessibility is tough.

Pick this if

The feeling matters as much as the function — and you’re willing to invest in graphics engineering.

Option 06

deck.gl + MapLibre + your own tile pyramid

Hard

The “my knowledge is Google Maps” path.

deck.gl MapLibre GL Supercluster tippecanoe Next.js

What it looks like

Treat your embedding UMAP as a coordinate system. Pre-render vector tiles (via tippecanoe) at zoom levels 0–14. Serve them as MVT. MapLibre renders them. deck.gl layers add the rich interactivity (hover cards, selection). Canvas lives inside a “building interior” layer that appears at max zoom.

Why pick it

Infinitely scalable rendering. Anyone who has ever used Google Maps knows the UX. Beautiful out of the box. The deck.gl + Mapbox pattern is battle-tested at Uber scale.

Where it hurts

Building tile pyramids is its own craft. Vector-tile styling is steep. Updating your corpus means regenerating tiles. For <100K items, this is overkill.

Pick this if

You’re thinking about a 1M+ item corpus (e.g. integrating OpenAlex), and you want no rendering ceiling ever.

Option 07

Obsidian vault + custom web viewer

Easy

The “editing is already solved, just add the map” path.

Obsidian Markdown files Dataview Quartz or Obsidian Publish A small embedding script

What it looks like

You write and edit everything in Obsidian. A Node/Python script embeds each note, clusters, regenerates labels. Quartz (open-source) publishes your vault as a static site, which you extend with a universe page that renders the clusters. Graph view is Obsidian’s native graph + an embedding map you add.

Why pick it

You inherit a polished editor and a whole plugin ecosystem. Your data is just Markdown on disk. Zero vendor lock-in.

Where it hurts

The web viewer has to re-implement several Obsidian behaviors (wikilinks, embeds, block refs). Obsidian’s aesthetic and your custom views won’t be in sync.

Pick this if

You already have (or want) an Obsidian vault and mostly want to share/visualize it online.

Option 08

Tauri desktop app, local-first

Medium

The “no server, no subscription, offline forever” path.

Tauri (Rust) React or Svelte front-end SQLite + sqlite-vec Local embedding model Ollama or llama.cpp for LLM

What it looks like

A native desktop app that runs entirely on your machine. Your corpus lives in a SQLite file. Embeddings run via sentence-transformers (CPU) or Ollama (GPU). Same React/Svelte UI as Option 2, just packaged natively.

Why pick it

Privacy by default. No per-token LLM bills. Your data never leaves your laptop. Deploys as a signed .app / .msi / .deb. Tauri bundles are ~10MB, Electron ~100MB.

Where it hurts

“Deployed online” was one of your explicit requirements — this isn’t that. Unless you add an optional sync layer, it’s personal-only. Building + signing for each OS is a chore.

Pick this if

Privacy and cost dominate, and you’d rather ship a desktop app than maintain a web service.

Option 09

Pure static site + Git-as-database

Easy

The “nothing to break” path.

Astro or 11ty Markdown in Git Precomputed JSON/Parquet GitHub Actions Deck.gl or Sigma

What it looks like

Every item is a Markdown file in a Git repo. A GitHub Action re-embeds, re-clusters, and publishes items.parquet + clusters.json whenever you push. The site reads those and renders. Editing = git commit.

Why pick it

Zero running infrastructure. Everything version-controlled. A researcher can understand the stack in a weekend. Forkable.

Where it hurts

Editing inside the site is hard to add. Git isn’t a real-time DB, so interactions that modify state round-trip through commits.

Pick this if

You’re comfortable with git as a writer’s medium and you’re optimizing for simplicity.

Option 10

Claude-native, LLM-first pipeline

Medium

The “the model does the organizing, not an algorithm” path.

Claude API Voyage embeddings Next.js Cosmograph Structured JSON output

What it looks like

Instead of HDBSCAN + c-TF-IDF, Claude proposes the cluster structure by reading the embedding neighborhoods. Claude names every cluster. Claude writes the “why these belong together” caption. Canvas auto-arranges itself via Claude’s suggested layout. You stay the final editor.

Why pick it

The labels become meaningful, not bag-of-words. The clustering respects semantics algorithms miss. When your corpus grows to include very different genres (papers + talks + notes), the model handles heterogeneity that statistics trip on.

Where it hurts

LLM bills. Non-determinism — the map shuffles unless you pin it. You have to design prompt and caching carefully.

Pick this if

Your corpus is heterogeneous and the quality of labels / groupings matters more than cost or speed.

Option 11

No-code stitched stack

Easy

The “ship in a weekend with existing tools” path.

Notion (editing) Kumu (graph) Heptabase (canvas) Super.so or Fathom (publishing)

What it looks like

Editing happens in Notion. Graph view lives in Kumu, imported from a Notion export. Canvas is Heptabase (deep-linkable). You stitch them under one subdomain via Super.so.

Why pick it

You ship today. Every tool is polished and professionally maintained. Good for testing whether the concept works before investing in code.

Where it hurts

Not unified. Different fonts, different keyboard shortcuts, different models. No semantic zoom at all. The embedding universe just isn’t in any of these tools.

Pick this if

You want to learn what you’d actually use before building anything custom.

Option 12

Python FastAPI + React (classic research stack)

Medium

The “a research engineer would build this” path.

FastAPI Postgres + pgvector UMAP/HDBSCAN/BERTopic React + D3 Docker

What it looks like

A proper backend service. FastAPI serves your embedding/cluster/label endpoints. pgvector handles similarity search. React front-end is your usual Next or Vite setup. Background jobs re-embed on change.

Why pick it

Python ML + Python ingestion + a real API. Everyone in your field can read this code. BERTopic and other PyData libraries plug in natively.

Where it hurts

You’re running a service. Docker, Postgres, auth, CORS, CI. The “deployed” requirement becomes “operated.” Fly.io, Railway, or Render soften this.

Pick this if

You’re already a Python researcher and prefer the tools you know — or you want this to be readable by your lab.

A recommended path

If you forced me to pick one, here’s the sequence

Week 0: Build Option 11 (no-code stitched) for one afternoon — just to feel what each level should do. Don’t keep it.

Weeks 1–2: Build Option 01 (Observable Framework + Deepscatter). Get your real corpus flowing through the real pipeline. Ship it as a personal reference site.

Months 1–3: If you love using the Observable version, migrate to Option 02 (Next.js + Cosmograph + tldraw). Most of your Python pipeline carries over; the front-end is the rewrite. This is your “real” tool.

Month 4+: Sprinkle Option 10 (LLM-first) on top of whatever you built — Claude-generated cluster labels, Claude-generated cross-level annotations, Claude-written “here’s what this region is” captions.

Three questions to decide between these paths

  1. How often will your corpus change? Hourly → need a running backend (02, 10, 12). Weekly or slower → static (01, 04, 09) wins.
  2. Does this need to be shareable? Public web → any of 01–07, 09–12. Just for you → Option 08 becomes very appealing.
  3. What matters most in the first screen? Readability → 04, 07. Density / scale → 02, 06. Awe → 05, 06. Interactivity → 02, 03.
A closing thought from this whole survey

The genre has been invented. The tools are all here. What doesn’t exist yet is your particular synthesis — embedding space + explicit graph + multi-entity types + editable + deployed + with your aesthetic. That’s the gap this site was assembled to help you close. None of the options above will build themselves. But each is a real, walked path — with collaborators, documentation, and examples you can read.
